Redesigning the Medicare shopping experience

Turning a content-heavy marketing page into a guided shopping journey — with AI-ready entry points, a simpler architecture, and a scalable foundation for personalization.

The challenge

The old homepage buried shopping actions under promos, education, videos and secondary CTAs — piling on cognitive load before anyone could start finding a plan. Product framed the ambition as good / better / best:

Increase plan discoveryAI-assisted shoppingUser segmentationBetter funnel dataPlan Companion visibilityOmnichannel-ready

Discovery & Research

To define the strategy, I started with a discovery phase focused on both the existing experience and the future direction of AI-assisted shopping.

01 · Current experience

Audited how users navigate the funnel today and where it breaks down.

IA & hierarchyEntry pointsClick pathsFriction pointsBusiness vs user goals

02 · Competitive analysis

Benchmarked other Medicare providers to see how the industry structures enrollment, communicates complex information, and drives discovery — surfacing conventions users already expect and openings to differentiate.

03 · Emerging AI experiences

Studied AI-adaptive products outside healthcare to learn how personalization is reshaping UX.

Personalized contentReal-time adaptationConversational guidancePredictive navigation
!

Key insight

AI-native experiences can cut decision complexity — but a fully AI-first homepage would be too big a leap for an audience used to traditional browsing. After exploring several AI-centered concepts, I aligned with Product on a phased adoption: AI as a complementary guidance layer, preserving familiar mental models while laying the foundation for future AI-driven personalization.

End-to-end user flows

The full shopping journey I mapped — opening, plan-specific paths, and enrollment

My approach

01Reframed around the goal

Instead of a marketing destination, I treated the homepage as the entry point of the shopping journey — cutting competing priorities and surfacing the highest-value actions immediately.

Plan types surfaced as scannable, self-select cards

02Reduced cognitive load

Every module used to carry equal visual weight. I flattened the noise by removing low-performing promos, cutting redundant education, consolidating navigation paths, and reducing scroll depth into a single progressive flow.

03Clearer information architecture

Content reorganized around user intent, not internal priorities — three progressive stages:

04AI-ready experience

While exploring AI-first interaction models, I partnered with Product to define an incremental adoption strategy rather than introducing a fully conversational experience immediately.

Guided by Jakob's Law, we preserved familiar shopping patterns while strategically introducing AI-assisted entry points — reducing adoption friction and creating a scalable path toward a future AI-centered experience.

AI-assisted entry points introduced alongside familiar patterns

05Built for personalization

Structured to support segmentation and shopper profiles — so the page adapts to the visitor instead of showing everyone the same thing. The same entry point flexes between a returning shopper and a first-time visitor:

Returning shopper — plans already surfaced

New visitor — guided to enter a ZIP

06Sharper conversion

A larger hero with a single dominant CTA, earlier ZIP-code entry, reduced CTA competition and consistent interaction patterns — one unmistakable path instead of competing buttons.

Impact

Shifted the homepage from a content-first experience to a conversion-focused shopping journey — lighter cognitive load, clearer hierarchy, and a scalable base for AI-powered personalization and omnichannel.

Going further

Progressive recommendation flow

As a continuation of the redesign, I explored how the primary CTA could evolve into a more guided, personalized journey — instead of redirecting on click, the recommendation card expands in place, preserving context and focus.

The recommendation card expands in place — three quick questions, no full enrollment flow

Within the expanded card, users answer three carefully selected questions about their needs and preferences. These build an initial shopper profile that filters the available plans before they even enter the shopping experience.

By capturing meaningful intent upfront, the flow shifts from browsing to guided discovery — lightweight and conversational, letting users make progress without committing to a long enrollment flow.
