Rebuilding the Medicare plan pages into one scalable system

Six high-traffic plan pages, a nine-year-old design, a legacy CMS and strict regulatory limits — redesigned into one consistent, scannable structure a user could learn once and reuse everywhere.

The context

Shop pages are the highest-traffic entry points into the plan-shopping funnel — SEO destinations, campaign landing pages and educational hubs at the same time, owned cross-functionally across teams. They hadn't seen a major redesign in nine years, and the numbers showed it.

What the data told us

Before touching a layout, I worked through analytics, the existing page inventory and the product team's performance review to understand where attention actually went — and where it stopped.

Where attention went

Engagement wasn't the problem — users spent an average of 5.3 minutes on these pages. The problem was what happened after the hero: interaction dropped off sharply, and the majority of clicks went to member sign-in rather than into shopping.

5.3 min avg. on page 60.8% to member sign-in Low CTA interaction beyond hero

What the design was missing

Auditing the six pages surfaced the same four gaps repeating on every one of them — and no shared structure to fix them once.

Static hero No personalization Weak CTA emphasis No quick navigation

Intent was already there

Ranking the actual interactions on plan pages showed users arriving with clear, practical intent — they wanted a recommendation, a drug cost estimate, an eligibility check.

Those tools existed. They were just buried below content that had accumulated over the years, competing for the same visual weight.

Designing inside the constraints

This project was shaped as much by what we couldn't do as by what we wanted to. Three hard limits defined every decision.

01 · Legacy platform

The pages run on AEM, a legacy CMS with a fixed component library. Rather than designing something the platform couldn't build, I adapted the design system to what AEM could actually render — reworking components, spacing and behaviours to stay within its authoring model while still feeling modern.

02 · Regulated content

Medicare content is legally governed — wording can't simply be simplified for clarity. I worked closely with a content designer to find language that was scannable and plain while staying compliant, treating copy as a design constraint from the first wireframe rather than a final step.

03 · The 25% rule

Any change above a 25% threshold triggers a full regulatory refile. Every decision had to be weighed against that ceiling — pushing me toward a system of reusable structure and components that could evolve incrementally instead of one large, risky redesign.

My approach

01One structure, six pages

Instead of designing six pages, I designed one repeatable structure. Each plan type answers the same underlying questions — what is this plan, who is it for, what does it cover, how do I enrol — so those answers should always live in the same place. A user who learns the pattern on one page can navigate any of the others without relearning it.

02Built to be scanned

With 85% of users never passing the fold, density was the enemy. I broke long regulatory copy into short, titled blocks, introduced jump links for quick navigation between plan categories and FAQs, and gave the hero collapsible behaviour so it stops occupying the whole first screen.

The goal wasn't less information — it was information a user could skim in seconds and drill into on demand.

03Actions where intent lives

The data was clear about what users wanted to do. I gave those actions — get a recommendation, compare plans, estimate drug costs, check eligibility — a consistent visual treatment and placed them at the points in the page where that intent naturally appears, not only at the top.

04Segmentation tiles

Four audiences arrive at these pages with very different needs: new shoppers, returning visitors, stalled shoppers and existing members. Rather than one generic page, I introduced segmentation tiles that let each group self-identify early and follow a path built for them.

It also addressed the sign-in problem — members finally had an obvious route of their own instead of absorbing the page's primary CTAs.

05Desktop-first, never mobile-last

72% of this traffic is desktop and 28% mobile — an unusual split that justified designing desktop-first. But an older audience on mobile is exactly the group least served by a cramped layout, so every component was specified with its responsive behaviour: how sections stack, how jump links collapse, how tiles reflow.

Rolling it out in three deliveries

Six pages couldn't ship at once — not with a refile threshold and a shared component library still being adapted. I sequenced the work so the first delivery established the system and each following one validated it against a harder case.

01

Medicare Hub · Medicare Advantage

The structural foundation. The Hub as the gateway into plan exploration and Advantage as the highest-traffic plan page — together they defined the pattern every other page would inherit.

02

Chronic & Dual Special Needs

The stress test. These plans carry the most eligibility conditions and the heaviest regulatory copy — proving the structure could absorb complexity without breaking.

03

Part D prescription drug plans

The final page, completing the ecosystem — by this point the system was mature enough that the page could be assembled from established components.

What I took from it

Designing within constraints is a core part of product design. This project challenged me to move beyond individual pages and create scalable patterns that balanced user needs, regulatory requirements, and technical feasibility.

By collaborating closely with Content Design and Engineering, I focused on creating flexible patterns that could improve the user experience while remaining realistic to build, maintain, and scale.
